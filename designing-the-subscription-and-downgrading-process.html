New upgrade and downgrade process for a SaaS product

ParseHub is a Software as a Service product making online data accessible to anyone, even without technical skills. We built the desktop data extraction app from scratch and it is free for anyone to use with basic features. Our main goal was to get people to pay a monthly subscription for extra features. The purchasing and also downgrading process was complete broken. This is how we fixed it.

BEFORE
  • People did not know we offer an enterprise plan. They did not scroll down enough.
  • When set to “Yearly Billing”, customers paid by accident and expected monthly billing. The support team spent extra time refunding.
  • Features were not explained. Customers contacted support to get common questions answered.
  • The “Schedule a Demo” button was sandwiched and lost in the design.
  • The “Enterprise” and “0-Hassle Plan” were too similar. We wanted people to contact sales but they were lost reading details instead.
  • Every plan asked customer to download the app. There was no call to action to purchase.
AFTER
  • Schedule a call" action is now more visible. When clicked, a pop-up asks the visitor to pick a time to chat - powered by Calendly.
  • Two custom plans were combined into "Enterprise". Moved above the fold beside the rest of the subscription plans.
  • The paid plans ask customer to buy and the free plan prompts to download the app.
  • Questions that people continuously asked support were added below the plans.
  • We made it clear that we will refund anyone no questions asked.
  • Previously unknown features were added to the plans and old features were made more understandble.

Making it Human

As soon as a customer purchased or cancelled their plan we sent them the two emails below.

Email: Thank you for subscribing

As soon as the customer subscribed to one of our paying plans we sent him or her an email. The email version to the left had a 63% open rate and 9% response rate. Most customers that subscribed were already in contact with our support team through other emails and live chat messages. This email was essential at grabbing the attention of customers that paid us money by themselves with zero contact.

Email: We are sorry to see you go

Right when a customer cancelled their plan we sent them an email asking them what we could have done better. The email version to the left had a 64% open rate and 14% response rate. After A/B testing a few different versions the email with one sentance performed better. When we asked the customer too many questions or said too many things in the email they ignored it more often.
